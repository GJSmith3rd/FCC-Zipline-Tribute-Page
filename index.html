Charles M Schulz

Creator of the Peanuts Comic Strip


Charles M. Schulz in 1956, drawing Charlie Brown

TIME LINE

  • 1922 - Born in Minneapolis, MN and grew up St. Paul, MN. Nicknamed Sparky.

  • 1943 - Served as a Staff Seargeant with the U.S. Army's 20th Armored Division in Europe.

  • 1945 - Provided lettering for the Roman Catholic comic magazine, Timeless Topix.

  • 1946 - Reviewed and graded lessons for students at Art Instruction, Incorporated.

  • 1947 - Authored a weekly series of one-panel jokes entitled, Li'l Folks, for the St. Paul Pioneer Press until 1950.

  • 1950 - Peanuts comic strip debuted in seven newspapers.

  • 1952 - Peanuts comic strip appeared in the weekly Sunday-page, eventually becoming one of the most popular comics strips in history.

  • 1955 - Recipient of National Cartoonists Society's, Reuben Award.

  • 1962 - Recipient of National Cartoonists Society's, Humo Comic Strip Award, for Peanuts.

  • 1964 - Recipient of National Cartoonists Society's, first 2nd time winner, Reuben Award.

  • 1980 - Recipient of National Cartoonists Society's, Elzie Segar Award.

  • 1981 - Recipient of Lester Patrick Trophy for outstanding contributions to the sport of hockey in the US.

  • 1993 - Inducted into the U.S. Hockey Hall of Fame.

  • 1996 - Honored with a star on the Hollywood Walk of Fame.

  • 1980 - Recipient of the National Cartoonists Society's, Elzie Segar Award.

  • 1999 - Recipient of the National Cartoonists Society's, Milton Caniff Lifetime Achievement Award.

  • 2000 - Died

  • 2001 - Awarded the U.S. Congressional Gold Medal, accepted by Shulz's wife Jean.

  • 2007 - Inducted into the U.S. Figure Skating Hall of Fame.

  • 2014 - Awarded the Harvey Kurtzman Hall of Fame Award, accepted by Karen Johnson, Director of the Charles M Schulz Museum.

"It would be impossible to narrow down three or two or even one direct influence on [Schulz's] personal drawing style. The uniqueness of Peanuts has set it apart for years... That one-of-kind quality permeates every aspect of the strip and very clearly extends to the drawing. It is purely his with no clear forerunners and no subsequent pretenders."
Biographer Rheta Grimsley Johnson
